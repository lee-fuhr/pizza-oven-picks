How to use a pizza oven

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To use a pizza oven, preheat the stone until the floor reaches around 700–800°F (the air will be hotter). Build a thin, lightly sauced pizza with room-temperature toppings, launch with a quick wrist flick using semolina on the peel, then turn every 30–60 seconds until the char is even. Let the stone recover between pies and clean only after it's fully cool.

How to preheat a pizza oven for perfect crust

The most common beginner mistake is rushing the preheat. The key is to heat both the stone floor and the dome or air above it. The stone needs to soak up heat for at least 30 to 45 minutes in a gas oven, longer if you’re using wood. You’re looking for a stone surface temperature of roughly 700 to 800°F; the air inside can be a couple hundred degrees higher. How do you tell without an infrared thermometer? A few classic cues: drop a pinch of flour on the stone and it should brown in about 10 to 15 seconds without smoking instantly. Or hold your hand a few inches above the stone, if you can’t keep it there for more than a couple seconds, you’re close. The stone will also begin to look slightly ashy or ‘live’ in the center. Once the whole cooking surface feels uniformly hot, you’re ready.

How to build a pizza that won't burn in a high-heat oven

High-heat pizza ovens cook a pizza in 60 to 90 seconds, so your ingredient strategy has to shift. Start with a thin, even dough, no more than about a quarter-inch thick in the center, a little thicker at the rim. Stretch it by hand, not with a rolling pin, to keep the air bubbles that create that blistered crust. Sauce goes on sparingly: a thin swirl, not a pool. Too much moisture turns the crust soggy before it crisps. Cheese should be a light, even layer, not a blanket; shred your own whole-milk mozzarella or use fresh mozzarella that’s been dried on paper towels for at least 10 minutes. Avoid cold toppings straight from the fridge, bring everything to room temperature. Cold cheese and meat cool the stone and lead to an underdone base. Less is more: three to four toppings max, and cut them small and thin.

How to launch a pizza without making a mess

The launch is where most first-timers lose their pizza. The trick is to build the pizza on a well-dusted wooden or metal peel. Use a 50/50 mix of fine semolina and rice flour, or just semolina, it rolls like tiny ball bearings under the dough. Sprinkle the peel generously, then shake off the excess before you start assembling. Before you add sauce, test the slide: give the peel a quick back-and-forth shake. If the dough sticks even a little, lift that edge and dust more semolina underneath. Once the pizza is topped, do the same shake test again. When you’re ready to launch, place the peel’s front edge on the stone at about a 20-degree angle, then pull the peel back sharply in one smooth motion. Don’t jerk; it’s a fast, confident flick. If you hesitate, the pizza will fold or dump its toppings. Practice on a cold countertop first.

How to turn a pizza and read the char pattern

Even the best home pizza ovens have hot spots, usually toward the back or the fire side. That’s why turning is essential. About 20 to 30 seconds in, lift a corner of the pizza with a turning peel to check the bottom, it should be pale golden with the first dark spots. Then rotate the pizza 90 to 180 degrees, depending on how aggressive the hot spot is. Use a small, round turning peel for this, not your launch peel. Slide it under the pizza, lift gently, and turn in one fluid motion. Most pizzas need two or three turns over the total cook time of about 90 seconds. Watch the rim: you want the cornicione to puff up with a few dark leopard spots, not char evenly. If one side is browning much faster, spend less time there on the next turn or pull the pizza slightly toward the cooler side of the stone. Trust your eyes, not a timer.

How to manage hot spots and keep the stone hot between pies

After you pull the first pizza, the stone’s surface temperature drops significantly, maybe 100 to 150°F. If you launch a second pizza immediately, it will likely stick and end up with a pale, doughy bottom. Give the oven at least 5 to 10 minutes to recover. During that time, rotate the stone’s position if your oven has a removable floor, or simply let the burner or fire run high. If you’re using wood, add a small split before you launch the first pizza so it catches and reheats the dome. In a gas oven, crank the burner to its highest setting for a couple of minutes, then dial back slightly before your next launch. Use an infrared thermometer to spot-check the stone: aim for a consistent reading across the cooking surface. If the back corner is 50°F hotter than the front, position your pizzas accordingly or use a baking steel as a heat distributor.

How to clean a pizza stone safely

Never wash a pizza stone with soap or put it in a dishwasher, it’s porous and will absorb soap, which then leaches into your next pizza. Instead, let the stone cool completely, then scrape off any burnt-on food with a stiff metal spatula or a purpose-built stone scraper. For stubborn residue, you can leave the oven on low (around 300°F) for 30 minutes to burn off excess oil or cheese, then scrape again after cooling. If the stone has deep stains or smells, a gentle scrub with a paste of baking soda and water works, but rinse thoroughly with plain water and let it dry completely before the next use. Avoid thermal shock: never pour cold water on a hot stone, or vice versa. Over time, small cracks are normal and won’t affect performance. A well-maintained stone will last for years and actually improve as it darkens.

Frequently asked questions

How do I know when the pizza stone is ready?

The best test is an infrared thermometer, aim for 700 to 800°F on the stone surface. Without one, sprinkle a pinch of flour on the stone; it should brown in about 10 to 15 seconds without burning immediately. The stone should also feel intensely hot when you hold your hand a few inches above it.

Should I use flour or semolina on the peel?

Semolina or a mix of semolina and rice flour works much better than all-purpose flour. The coarse grains act like tiny ball bearings that let the dough slide easily. All-purpose flour can turn gummy under the moisture of the dough and cause sticking.

Why does my pizza stick to the peel?

Most likely you didn’t dust enough semolina, or you let the topped pizza sit too long before launching, moisture from the sauce and cheese seeps into the dough and creates a sticky seal. Build your pizza quickly, test the slide before you add sauce, and launch within a minute of finishing the toppings.

How often should I rotate the pizza?

Plan on turning the pizza every 20 to 30 seconds during the roughly 90-second cook. That gives you two to three rotations total. The first turn is the most important because hot spots char fast. Use a small turning peel and rotate 90 to 180 degrees depending on how uneven the heat is.

Why is my pizza burnt on the bottom but raw on top?

That’s a sign the stone is too hot relative to the air temperature, or the pizza is sitting too close to the flame. Raise the pizza slightly off the stone with a small wire rack or use a turning peel to lift it for a few seconds to let the top catch up. Also, check that your dough isn’t too thick or wet.

Can I put cold toppings straight from the fridge?

Avoid it. Cold toppings cool the stone and cause a soggy, undercooked base. Let cheese, meats, and vegetables come to room temperature for about 30 minutes before assembling. If you’re using fresh mozzarella, pat it dry and let it air-dry on paper towels for at least 15 minutes.

In shortMastering the launch and turn takes a few tries, but once you feel that confident wrist flick and see an evenly leopard-spotted crust, you’ll never go back to delivery. Start with a simple Margherita and dial in your technique, the best pizza you’ll ever eat is the one that comes out of your own oven.
